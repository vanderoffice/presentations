A.1: E3 Role Definitions

E3 Undersecretary for Efficiency and Effectiveness

Classification Career Executive Assignment (CEA) Level B/C
Reports To Dual: Agency Secretary + TMO Director
Salary Range $180,000 - $250,000 + benefits
Count 8-12 positions (one per major agency)

Key Responsibilities

  • Develop and execute agency modernization roadmap
  • Oversee all IT investments exceeding $2M
  • Chair agency Data Governance Council
  • Approve data sharing agreements (Type 2-3)
  • Report quarterly to TMO on portfolio performance
  • Mentor and supervise Chief Deputy Directors for E&E
TEA Requirement: E3 Undersecretaries with technology portfolios must meet Pathway 2 (Experience) or Pathway 3 (Panel) qualification requirements.

A.5: Data Governance Decision Tiers

Decision Level Authority Scope Example
Department CDD + Data Stewards Public data within agency; non-sensitive operational DMV shares aggregate vehicle data with Air Resources Board
Agency E3 Undersecretary Confidential data with limited PII within agency HHS shares licensing data between departments
Cross-Agency Independent Office Board Sensitive PII, cross-agency, cross-branch Corrections shares inmate data with Social Services

Escalation Path

  1. Agency-internal disputes: Undersecretary resolves with Data Steward support
  2. Cross-agency operational: TMO facilitates between Undersecretaries
  3. Cross-agency sensitive: Independent Office Board provides binding decision
  4. Cross-branch: Independent Office Board (includes judicial, legislative representation)

B.7: Department Readiness Assessment Framework

Six domains scored 1-5 to determine deployment priority:

DomainWeightIndicators
Leadership Commitment 25% Director support, designated sponsor, resource commitment
Technical Foundation 20% IT infrastructure maturity, data quality, API readiness
Change Capacity 15% Prior modernization experience, staff adaptability
Stakeholder Alignment 15% Union relations, external partner support
Business Case 15% Savings potential, citizen impact, audit findings
Risk Profile 10% Complexity, dependencies, political sensitivity
Scoring: Departments scoring 4.0+ are "ready" for immediate E3 deployment. Departments scoring 3.0-3.9 receive preparation support. Below 3.0 are deferred until capacity improves.

C.1: EDD Fraud Case Study

By the Numbers

Pandemic fraud $20B+
Ongoing annual fraud $500M+
Improper payments (2023-24) $1.5B
Years on high-risk list 3+

Root Causes

  • Legacy systems with no real-time verification
  • Manual identity verification processes
  • No cross-agency data sharing
  • Deferred technology investment
  • Insufficient fraud analytics capability

"its estimated rate of fraudulent payments remains above the level it was in 2019, before the pandemic, indicating that the amount of fraud was more than $500 million in 2024 alone"

‚Äî California State Auditor Report 2025-601

Key Insight: The pandemic didn't create EDD's vulnerabilities‚Äîit exposed them. The same systemic issues that enabled $20B in pandemic fraud continue to enable $500M+ in annual losses today.

C.2: FI$Cal Case Study

Project Timeline

Initiated2005 (Schwarzenegger)
Original estimate$617M (2012)
Current spending$1.06B+
Years elapsed20 years
StatusStill incomplete

Administrations Spanned

  • Schwarzenegger (2005-2011) ‚Äî Initiated
  • Brown (2011-2019) ‚Äî Continued
  • Newsom (2019-present) ‚Äî Ongoing

Three governors, multiple scope changes, repeated cost overruns

What Went Wrong

  • Waterfall procurement: massive contract, single vendor, years between milestones
  • No modular contracting: couldn't pivot when requirements changed
  • No transition protection: each administration reset priorities
  • Accountability theater: oversight existed but lacked enforcement
How E3 Addresses This: $15M modular cap prevents mega-contracts. Civil service E3 positions survive transitions. Stage-gate reviews enable early termination of failing modules.

C.3: CDT High-Risk Status History

Years on State Auditor High-Risk List

Why Nothing Has Changed

  • No enforcement authority: Auditor can only recommend, not mandate
  • Self-reporting: SLAA requires agencies to grade themselves
  • Advisory bodies: Little Hoover Commission "empowered only to make recommendations"
  • No consequences: Same issues identified year after year with no accountability
The Question: If 18 years of recommendations haven't worked, what makes us think 19 years will be different? The answer: it won't. We need enforcement authority, not more recommendations.

C.5: Colorado OIT Model

Structure

  • Centralized Office of Information Technology
  • Statewide CIO with cabinet-level authority
  • Unified IT budget: $373M
  • Shared services model across agencies

Results

  • Consolidated 20 data centers to 2
  • Standardized security across agencies
  • Unified identity management
  • Enterprise vendor agreements

Applicability to California

Centralized CIO California has State CIO, but without E3 enforcement authority
Shared services CDT offers shared services, but adoption is voluntary
Enterprise contracts Proposed Vendor Pools achieve similar effect
Key Difference: Colorado is smaller (16 agencies vs. California's 200+ departments). E3 model adapts centralized principles to federated reality‚Äîauthority at agency level with statewide coordination.

C.6: Texas DIR Model

Department of Information Resources

Strengths

  • Statewide contracts save 20-30%
  • Centralized cybersecurity operations
  • IT workforce development programs
  • Enterprise architecture standards

Limitations

  • Advisory, not enforcement authority
  • Agency adoption remains voluntary
  • No data governance authority
  • Decentralized IT spending decisions

What California Can Learn

  • Enterprise contracts work: Adopt Vendor Pool model for similar savings
  • Cybersecurity benefits from centralization: CISO authority in E3 model
  • Advisory isn't enough: E3 provides authority Texas DIR lacks

E.2: Rapid RFI¬≤ Guide Overview

When to Use RFI¬≤

  • Problem is clear but best solution is uncertain
  • Multiple viable approaches exist
  • Traditional requirements would constrain innovation
  • AI/emerging technology applications

The 4-Phase Process

Phase 1: Problem Statement 2-3 weeks Define problem, outcomes, success criteria
Phase 2: Concept Papers 2 weeks Vendors submit 5-page concepts; shortlist 2-3
Phase 3: Paid Sprint 4-5 weeks $50K each for working prototypes
Phase 4: Evaluation 1 week Live demos; award contract
Key Principle: The code is the proposal. Evaluate working software, not written promises.

E.4: Journey Teams Guide Overview

What Are Journey Teams?

Cross-functional teams that follow a citizen's experience across agencies to identify friction points and design improvements.

Journey Team Composition

Core Team

  • Product owner
  • UX researcher
  • Service designer
  • Data analyst

Extended Team

  • Agency subject matter experts
  • Frontline staff representatives
  • Technology specialists

Stakeholders

  • E3 Undersecretaries
  • Agency leadership
  • Citizen advisors

Journey Team Process

  1. Map: Document current citizen experience across touchpoints
  2. Measure: Quantify pain points, wait times, handoffs
  3. Ideate: Generate improvement opportunities
  4. Prototype: Test solutions with real users
  5. Implement: Quick Wins first, Strategic Initiatives for larger changes
